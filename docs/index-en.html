Driven by measured atmospheric data, ray-by-ray tracing through millions of paths — every pixel of the Moon's color is computed, not photographed.

During a total lunar eclipse, a thin cyan band hugs the edge of the Moon.

That band has a name: the turquoise fringe. It sits between the coppery red of the blood Moon and the bright white of the full Moon, appearing as an extremely faint cyan layer. Instead of photographing it to prove it's there, we went the other way: using measured atmospheric data, we ray-traced the refraction of millions of sunlight rays passing through Earth's atmosphere and computed this band from first principles. The process broke several times, and each failure forced us to fill in a missing piece of physics. At the end, we gained something no photograph can give you: the ability to switch ozone off inside the model and see whether the band disappears.

Three counterintuitive things. You'll understand them by the time you finish this page.

Ozone makes the turquoise fringe — but it's only half the story.

Why is it a narrow band? Why doesn't the whole inner umbral edge turn cyan?

The most common science-outreach explanation blames ozone's Chappuis absorption band: it gobbles up orange-red sunlight between 500 and 650 nm, leaving blue-green light to pass through. The chemistry is correct, but it can't explain one crucial observation — the color shift that ozone produces spans a smooth gradient over a dozen arcminutes across the lunar disk. If you looked at color alone, the entire umbral boundary should appear as a broad pale-cyan wash. The band looks narrow because of brightness, not color. The Brightness Cliff section below finishes this story.

Why does the turquoise fringe vanish at the deepest moment of totality?

Deep inside the umbra, the sunlight grazing Earth passes through the lower atmosphere. Down there, Rayleigh scattering strips away blue light completely — atmospheric molecules scatter shorter wavelengths far more efficiently — so only red light punches through. Cyan emerges higher up: when the grazing altitude climbs into the stratosphere, ozone's color control overpowers Rayleigh scattering, and blue-green light finally gets a chance to leak through. On the Moon, this altitude window maps right onto the umbral edge. So when the blood Moon is at its deepest, reddest point, the turquoise fringe is actually waiting just outside the lunar disk — it never got the invitation.

Why do lunar eclipse photos online look so much bluer?

Satellite measurements give red-to-blue ratios between 0.8 and 1.0. What that actually looks like is an extremely faint cyan tint — borderline invisible to the naked eye. Those online images where half the disk sits in saturated blue are the product of post-processing: capture multiple exposures at different settings, stack them into a single HDR composite, then crank the cyan channel saturation way up in the HSL panel. We verified this against the physics — next section digs in.

First faceplant: treating a viral photo as ground truth

That half-disk-bathed-in-saturated-blue look — we couldn't make it happen, no matter what we tried.

Midway through the project, we came across a widely circulated eclipse photo with a blue band: half the lunar disk submerged in a dense, saturated blue. We used that as a reference and kept re-running the model, but all we ever got was a faint cyan wash. At one point we genuinely suspected we'd overlooked some physical mechanism. Then we traced the photo back to its source — the author is a landscape-photography post-processing tutor, and the post's title literally spells out the editing pipeline: HDR stacking across multiple exposures, then pulling the cyan channel saturation up in HSL until it hits near-neon territory. The same kind of creator has even said in tutorials that this blue is nearly imperceptible to the naked eye and only shows up once HDR drags out colors that would otherwise go unnoticed.

On the physics side, the clues pointed in another direction. GOES-16 weather satellite measurements during lunar eclipses give red-to-blue ratios in the 0.8 to 1.0 range. In unprocessed professional astrophotography frames, the blue band is nothing more than a thin pale-cyan ribbon clinging to the Moon's edge. We never altered any physical parameter to chase that viral photo, and the measured data has since lined up with our results, piece by piece.

Amusingly, our own model did briefly spit out that intense cyan. When we treated the Sun as a point source — a simplification, of course — the model produced a band that was vivid and sharp, with the bluest point hitting an R/B ratio as low as 0.41. The moment we restored the Sun's real 32′ angular-diameter disk, the intense cyan got smeared out into a soft-edged pale cyan, the R/B ratio retreated to 0.71, and the result matched real photos. In other words, that viral-photo blue really does exist in an imaginary point-source world — it's just that our Sun is not a point, and it doesn't play along.

Human vision adds another layer. The band's absolute chromaticity really is blue-leaning: no matter how you tune the model — remove Rayleigh scattering, add aerosols, simulate the backfill from multiple scattering — it never turns teal. The reason people perceive it as cyan or greenish is the massive field of warm red from the blood Moon next to it; once your visual system chromatically adapts to that warm surround, a relatively cooler band reads as cyan-green. "Turquoise" is a relative color. We never bolted any extra mechanism onto the model to make it turn green in an absolute sense.

Same physics, flipped perspective

Stand on the Moon and look at Earth — you're watching a total solar eclipse.

During a total lunar eclipse, if you stand on the lunar surface and look toward Earth, the planet's night side completely covers the Sun, leaving only a ring of atmosphere-lit glow circling Earth's limb. These two viewpoints are two expressions of the same radiative-transfer physics: the color at any point on the Moon equals the color of the atmospheric ring you'd see looking up from that spot. The turquoise fringe at the Moon's edge corresponds to a small cyan segment on the sunward side of Earth's atmospheric ring, at stratospheric altitude.

The faceplant that decided everything

The band is narrow because of brightness.

For a long time, we tunneled on the color dimension, obsessing over how wide the band really is. The literature says it spans only a few percent of the lunar disk diameter, but our renders showed it sprawled across nearly a third of the Moon. So we rechecked ozone parameters, geometry, color science — over and over. It took us a while to realize the failure was in exposure. Our early renders artificially boosted umbral brightness to make the dark regions visible. The blue zone is already quite dim; once you yank the brightness up, it spreads into a broad gradient band. This wasn't a physics mistake — it was a display-strategy artifact.

The real mechanism is a brightness cliff. The band's bluest point sits right in a luminance trough. Immediately next to it, the region that transitions toward normal bright moonlight starts receiving direct sunlight from the penumbra, and the brightness shoots up violently. On our model card, photopic surface luminance climbs from 10% to 90% of the full Moon's value over just 20.4′ of angular distance (from 46.7′ to 67.1′); at the steepest part of the umbral boundary, the brightness gradient hits 1.8 stops per arcminute. The turquoise fringe you can actually see is the tiny intersection where two conditions hold simultaneously — the color is blue enough and the brightness is high enough — squeezed into a narrow sliver by this cliff. The color gradient along the disk is smooth, and what you actually see is a sharp-edged band. These two facts are not in conflict.

This faceplant left us with two takeaways. First, your choice of exposure and tone-mapping strategy is fully capable of misleading you about the underlying physics: when a render looks wrong, the first thing to do is separate whether the physics is wrong from whether the display is lying to you. Second, this brightness curve also answers why there's no turquoise fringe at the deepest moment of totality: the umbral core sits more than ten stops below full-Moon brightness, the sunlight's grazing altitude is too low for ozone to matter, and cyan only lives in that narrow slit along the brightness cliff.

Scientific boundaries

This model has a defined domain of applicability. Three open issues are laid out plainly here.

What matched, what didn't — all on the table

Everything that lines up and everything that doesn't, transparently laid out.

Three things match. First, the computed refraction geometry deviates from Mallama 2021's tabulated values by less than 2% across the mid-to-high grazing altitudes where the band lives. Second, the band's R/B ratio at its bluest point falls in the same ballpark as satellite measurements — both in the 0.8–1.0 pale-cyan range (the two measurements use different passbands; a detailed item-by-item reconciliation is documented in the second open issue below). Third, the band hugs the umbral boundary, appears pale cyan, and is narrow — consistent with what's seen in unprocessed professional astrophotography frames.

The first mismatch lies at the dark end. Our model's umbral center measures roughly −15 stops, a figure built on the clearest possible sky conditions with the addition of climatological background aerosol (pure molecular atmosphere with zero aerosol gives a theoretical upper bound of −13.5 stops). Mallama's clear-sky empirical extinction model reports the umbral center at −18.7 stops (disk-integrated) to −20.5 stops (resolved-disk V-band). Both models are called "clear-sky," but their results differ by several stops. One root cause is that "clear-sky" does not mean the same thing across the two models: the slant path of grazing sunlight through Earth's atmosphere corresponds to 40–60 airmasses, so even a tiny difference in background-aerosol values gets amplified massively. This discrepancy remains open.

The second mismatch involves satellite narrowband measurement passbands. Shu 2024, using the GF-4 satellite, captured a narrow ribbon with R/B < 1 at the umbral boundary, with a radial full width of 120–190 km. We re-ran our model under the same passband and couldn't reproduce this ribbon: single-layer physics can generate a narrow-band dip, but the 32′ solar disk's convolution effect and the red light flowing in from the umbral core wipe it out. We ran a sensitivity analysis that ruled out aerosol parameters and deep red light as suspects in turn. The strongest candidate explanation at this point is that the red shoulder light in the 8–19 km tangent-altitude range lacks sufficient extinction — a possible source being thin cirrus at the tropopause, which our model does not include, while the actual cloud conditions on the night of that eclipse are no longer recoverable. On top of that, three passband differences are in play: differences in satellite spectral response, ±15% lunar-albedo variation, and the mismatch between 2D boundary measurement and 1D radial averaging. Any single one of these three, taken alone, is enough to flip the verdict.

The third item is an explicit negative boundary the model claims for itself: it cannot predict whether the next lunar eclipse will look bright or dark. That depends on stratospheric aerosol conditions at the time of the eclipse — a total lunar eclipse following a major volcanic eruption can be several stops darker than one during a quiet year. The model provides the physical mechanism and a brightness floor under the clearest conditions; it is not a forecasting tool.

For those who want to dig deeper

How were these images computed?

These images don't rely on texture-map color grading, and no phenomenological fitting parameters were stuffed in anywhere. Refraction is computed by numerical integration of every single ray; extinction is accumulated along the bent path using measured cross-section data; ray focusing and defocusing emerge from the landing-point density of millions of rays striking the lunar disk.
